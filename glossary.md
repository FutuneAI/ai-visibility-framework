# Glossary: AI Visibility and Generative Engine Optimization

**Repository:** ai-visibility-framework
**Path:** /glossary.md
**Version:** 1.0

## Core Concepts

**AI Visibility**
The degree to which a brand, product, or organization is accurately and proactively represented in responses generated by large language model systems. Distinct from search visibility (Google rankings) and social visibility (platform reach). Measured across four states: Complete Absence, Partial/Inaccurate Representation, Accurate but Passive, and Active Recommendation Presence.
→ See: [what-is-ai-visibility.md](./phase-1-ai-visibility/01-what-is-ai-visibility.md)

**Entity**
In AI and knowledge graph terminology, a discrete, identifiable thing that can be represented as a node in a knowledge structure — a person, organization, product, place, or concept. When an AI system "knows" a brand, it maintains an entity representation of that brand: its name, category, attributes, and relationships to other entities.

**Entity Definition**
The process of ensuring a brand's entity representation in AI systems is accurate, complete, and consistent across all information sources. Poor entity definition leads to fragmented, inaccurate, or absent AI representation.
→ See: [entity-definition.md](./components/entity-definition.md)

**Parametric Knowledge**
Information encoded into a language model's parameters during training. Parametric knowledge is static after training — it does not update when a website changes or new content is published. Distinguished from retrieved knowledge, which is acquired dynamically at inference time.

**Training Data Coverage**
The extent to which a brand is represented in the corpus used to train a language model. Higher training data coverage — more mentions, across more independent sources, with greater clarity and consistency — produces stronger parametric knowledge of the brand.

## Retrieval and Technical Terms

**RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)**
An architectural pattern in which an AI system retrieves relevant document fragments from an external index before generating a response. Retrieved fragments are injected into the model's context window as additional information. Perplexity AI is a prominent example of a RAG-dominant system. Distinguished from pure parametric generation (ChatGPT without browsing).
→ See: [retrieval-mechanisms.md](./model/retrieval-mechanisms.md)

**Embedding / Vector Embedding**
A numerical representation of text in a high-dimensional space, produced by an embedding model. Semantically similar texts produce similar embeddings (vectors that are "close" in the high-dimensional space). RAG retrieval works by comparing the embedding of a query to the embeddings of stored document chunks and returning the closest matches.

**Semantic Distance**
A measure of how similar two pieces of text are in meaning, as represented by their embedding vectors. Lower semantic distance = more similar meaning. RAG retrieval selects the document chunks with the smallest semantic distance to the query. Generic content has high semantic distance from specific queries; specific, dense content has low semantic distance from relevant queries.

**Semantic Density**
A property of content referring to the amount of specific, distinct, verifiable information contained per unit of text. High-density content contains specific claims, data, named entities, standards, applications, and differentiated positions. Low-density content contains generic statements, abstract claims, and interchangeable language. High-density content is more likely to be retrieved by RAG systems for relevant queries.
→ See: [semantic-density.md](./components/semantic-density.md)

**Chunking**
The process of dividing a document into smaller segments (chunks) for storage in a retrieval index. Each chunk is independently embedded and retrievable. Chunk quality — whether each chunk is coherent and information-complete when read in isolation — affects retrieval performance.

**Retrieval Candidacy**
Whether a piece of content is in a position to be retrieved by a RAG system for relevant queries. Requires: (1) the content is indexed by the retrieval system, (2) the content has sufficient semantic relevance to be retrieved, (3) technical factors (crawlability, format) don't prevent indexing.

**Probabilistic Sampling**
The stochastic process by which language models generate responses. Models do not deterministically produce a single "correct" response — they sample from a probability distribution over possible next tokens. This means AI visibility is inherently probabilistic: a well-represented brand has higher mention probability, not guaranteed mention.

## SEO and GEO Terms

**SEO (Search Engine Optimization)**
The practice of optimizing web content and technical factors to improve ranking in search engine results pages (primarily Google). A foundational layer for AI visibility — Google-indexed content is a significant source of LLM training data, and high-authority domains tend to rank higher in retrieval systems — but insufficient alone for AI visibility.

**GEO (Generative Engine Optimization)**
The practice of optimizing content, entity definition, and external presence specifically for visibility in AI-generated responses. GEO operates at a different layer than SEO: where SEO optimizes for deterministic ranking signals (keywords, links, authority scores), GEO optimizes for probabilistic retrieval and representation in AI systems. GEO is the fourth layer above SEO → AEO → LLMO in the optimization hierarchy.

**AEO (Answer Engine Optimization)**
Optimization aimed at appearing in direct-answer features of search engines (featured snippets, Knowledge Panels, "People Also Ask" boxes). An intermediate layer between SEO and GEO — AEO focuses on structured, question-answering content that search engines surface directly, without requiring a click.

**LLMO (Large Language Model Optimization)**
Optimization specifically for how brands are represented in large language model parametric knowledge. Focuses on training data coverage — ensuring that sufficient, accurate, consistent information about a brand exists in the sources that LLMs train on. The third layer in the SEO → AEO → LLMO → GEO hierarchy.

**AI-Native Distribution**
An emerging fifth layer above GEO, referring to strategies for entering the source pools that AI systems draw on upstream — before retrieval happens. Includes: inclusion in vertical knowledge bases, citation by AI search structured data sources, coverage in enterprise AI knowledge bases. Distinct from GEO in that GEO optimizes existing web content for retrieval, while AI-native distribution addresses inclusion in the source pools themselves.

**Schema.org**
A collaborative vocabulary for structured data markup on web pages, supported by Google, Microsoft, Yahoo, and Yandex. Schema.org markup (typically implemented as JSON-LD) provides machine-readable entity definition directly on web pages, helping AI systems correctly attribute content to specific entities. Not a retrieval lever but an attribution and entity consolidation tool.
→ See: [structured-data.md](./components/structured-data.md)

**llms.txt**
An emerging convention for a plain-text file at the domain root that describes a website's content structure for AI systems. Analogous to robots.txt in format but intended for AI agents rather than search crawlers. Not yet standardized; low-weight signal currently but growing in adoption.

**sameAs**
A Schema.org property that explicitly links an entity described on one web page to the same entity on other platforms (LinkedIn, Wikipedia, Wikidata, etc.). Critical for entity consolidation across sources — without it, AI systems may maintain separate representations of the same brand.

## Diagnostic Terms

**Complete Absence (State 1)**
AI visibility state in which the AI system has no usable representation of the brand. Brand does not appear in category queries; direct queries produce disclaimers, confabulation, or entity confusion.

**Partial/Inaccurate Representation (State 2)**
AI visibility state in which the AI system has a representation of the brand but it contains errors — wrong category, wrong geography, wrong capabilities, or confusion with another entity.

**Accurate but Passive (State 3)**
AI visibility state in which the AI system can describe the brand accurately when queried directly, but the brand does not surface proactively in category-level or recommendation queries. The most common state for established brands that have not actively built AI visibility.

**Active Recommendation Presence (State 4)**
AI visibility state in which the brand surfaces proactively in category-level queries without being named. The target state. Probabilistic, not absolute — a State 4 brand appears consistently across patterns of relevant queries, not in every individual response.

**Structural Assessment**
Evaluation of the underlying properties that predict AI visibility outcomes — entity definition clarity, content semantic density, external citation network, retrieval infrastructure — without querying AI systems directly. The basis of the visibility diagnostic.

**Behavioral Testing**
Direct querying of AI systems to observe whether and how a brand appears in responses. Complements structural assessment but subject to probabilistic variance — patterns across many tests are more reliable than individual results.

## Platform Terms

**Perplexity AI**
AI search engine using a RAG-dominant architecture. Retrieves real-time sources for most queries. The platform most directly influenced by current content quality and crawlability — and therefore the highest-leverage platform for brands with strong current content but weak historical training coverage.

**ChatGPT Search**
Browsing-enabled mode of ChatGPT that supplements parametric knowledge with real-time Bing-based retrieval. Influenced by both training data coverage and current web content quality.

**Gemini**
Google's large language model, available in standard (training-dominant) and Search-grounded (RAG-enabled) variants. AI Overviews in Google Search use Search-grounded Gemini. Closely tied to Google SEO signals in its retrieval layer.

**Microsoft Copilot**
Microsoft's AI assistant, integrated in Bing and Microsoft 365. Uses Bing-based retrieval for web information. Enterprise Copilot may access internal knowledge bases — a separate channel from public web retrieval.
